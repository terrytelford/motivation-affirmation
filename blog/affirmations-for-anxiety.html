Affirmations for Anxiety: What the Research Says vs. What Actually Helps

May 30, 2026 · MOTIVATION AFFIRMATION

If you’ve ever tried to talk yourself out of anxiety with a positive statement and had it make things worse, you’re not alone and you’re not doing it wrong. Anxiety has a specific relationship with positive self-talk that’s different from how it responds to other kinds of cognitive reframing — and understanding the difference explains why some affirmations help and others backfire.

The problem with “I am calm”

When you’re anxious, your nervous system is in a state of perceived threat. Saying “I am calm” while your heart is racing and your thoughts are spiraling doesn’t calm the nervous system — it creates a contradiction that the nervous system reads as additional information to process. Research by Joanne Wood at the University of Waterloo found that self-affirming positive statements can actually increase negative mood in people with low self-esteem or in states of emotional distress — exactly the population most likely to be reaching for an anxiety affirmation.

What works instead: acceptance framing

The affirmations that actually reduce anxiety are the ones that don’t try to replace the anxious feeling — they acknowledge it while reframing the relationship to it. Compare:

Doesn’t work: “I am calm and at peace.” (Your body knows this isn’t true right now.)

Works better: “I can be anxious and still handle this.” (True. Has been true before. Your body can’t argue.)

The second version doesn’t try to eliminate the anxiety. It removes the anxiety about being anxious — which is often what’s actually making things worse.

Grounding affirmations vs. aspiration affirmations

For anxiety specifically, grounding affirmations outperform aspiration affirmations almost every time. A grounding affirmation is any statement anchored to present reality: “I have handled situations like this before.” “Right now, in this moment, I am safe.” “My nervous system is doing its job. I don’t have to fix it — I just have to let it run.”

These work because they give the nervous system accurate information rather than contradictory information. The nervous system responds to evidence. Give it evidence.

The breath connection

The most effective anxiety affirmation practice pairs the statement with a physiological intervention. Say your grounding affirmation on the exhale of a slow, extended breath (five counts in, seven counts out). The extended exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system directly — and the affirmation anchors to that physical shift rather than fighting the anxiety independently.
